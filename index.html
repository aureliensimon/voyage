About This Journey

I was first supposed to do this international journey way earlier, but then... pandemic. Which made very difficult since borders were all closed and furthermore the situation wasn't getting easy to find somewhere to sleep, somewhere to work. So few years passed with internship and apprenticeship, I decided to make some months to finally do this journey that was on my mind for a long time. For the destination I already knew where I wanted to go, well to be fair there was two, Germany and Ireland were the two choices for me, and since I went for several weeks in Germany already a few years back I decided to choose Ireland for this one. The decision was also based on my currents needs, because even tho a lot of german can speak very good english, in order to improve mine, I prefer to live in a native english-speaking country. So now that we know where we're going, let's pack our bag and leave France for some time !

My goal was to go alone (without any friend of family with me), to be fully emerge in the native population and with the strangers I will met on the way. It was a way to get out of my confort zone and we'll discuss here on how it went !

Cultural Discoveries & Personal Growth

What This Experience Taught Me

Language Adaptation

First advice is : don't put too much pression over yourself. Going in a native speaking english might seems overwhelming and all you can think of is how you're not going to understand every sentence that's going to be said to you or that you won't be able to say a word without pausing for a whole minut to find the next word you want to say. Irish people are notably patient with language learners compared to other anglophone countries, so don't be afraid to make mistakes while talking, the worst you can have a is laugh because the sentence sounded funny to them. You will also notice that once you think you've mastered the irish accent, in reality you only mastered the Cork's accent, and find yourself learning from sratch the Belfast's sharper accent, you have to treat each as a distinct dialect with fresh attention, and just like the tone of the voice, the speed varies wildly, rural speaks often talk way faster than Dubliners, and I'm not even talking about west coast ! You will also learn some "grand"/"craic" and more special words like this, a very unique way to improve your local vocabulary !

ü§ù

Social Openness

While in France conversations with strangers can feel formal or reserved, here is Ireland it's night and day, Irish people strike up conversations at bus stops, shops, anywhere. "Where are you from?" "What brings you here?" within minutes of knowing each other, they are not getting instrusive or investigating on you but it's friendly interest. When you go outside in a pub for example, sharing tables with strangers is normal. If you decide to be closed and refuse to chat when someone sits nearby, you WILL appears hostile. And don't hesitate to show fully yourself, french's tendency to stay reserved can be reads as unfriendly, and showing enthusiasm/disappointment/surprise is expected and very appreciated !

üéØ

Adaptability

Technicals details here : Cash is still king rurally, unlike card-everywhere France, many rural pubs/shops are cash-only and ATMs are genuinely 20km+ apart in Donegal/Mayo. And always be prepared for the next day in each scenario, because irish rain vocabulary is extensive and minimizing : a "soft day" mean steady rain and "grand stretch" can assume a brief sun between downpours, so carry rain gear always, even in sunshine, the atlantic coast is especially unpredictable there. So plan, but relax, booking ahead for example is less critical than France, locals will find you somewhere to sleep is you really need too. Try as much to focus on what you can control and let the human interactions do the rest !

Relationship with Time

First thing you need to understand: "I'll be there in five minutes" in Ireland is not a promise, it's an optimistic aspiration. Coming from France where being 10 minutes late requires apologies and a valid excuse, you'll find yourself waiting 20-30 minutes past the agreed time and nobody bats an eye. "Irish time" is real and fighting it will only frustrate you. Meetings operate differently too - "let's meet Tuesday" without specifying morning or afternoon is completely normal, you're expected to text day-of to confirm the actual time. Business meetings start with 15 minutes of genuine small talk before anyone mentions work, rushing straight to the agenda reads as rude and transactional. You will also discover "island time" becomes even more elastic the further west you go, Connemara operates on an entirely different temporal plane than Dublin, and trying to schedule three things in one day out there is adorably naive to locals.

Cultural Awareness

Ireland's culture hits different once you stop trying to decode it through a French lens and just let it wash over you. Hospitality here isn't the polite-but-distant French courtesy where you'd never dream of bothering someone. It's genuine ! See by yourself : I once asked for directions to a shop in Sligo and ended up at a family dinner three hours later, meeting cousins and being fed until I couldn't move. That's just Tuesday here. The Garda√≠ too, they're nothing like the French police's formal distance‚Äîthey'll stop their patrol car to help you jumpstart your vehicle or have a proper chat about the weather, treating you like a neighbor who just happens to need directions !

Independence

Going solo was terrifying at first, not going to lie. I felt like losing every safety nets I had : My friends, my neighborhood caf√©, my predictable routine, etc. And suddenly you're dopped there and every single decision was on me. Where am I sleeping tonight? How do I get there? What do I do when the plan I spent hours crafting completely falls apart? In Kilkenny, I missed the last bus by literally two minutes and had to scramble for a hostel with zero booking, my phone at 12% battery, frantically messaging every place that looked remotely available. In Connemara, I tackled a solo hike through terrain that looked way easier on the map, no signal, just me and my questionable sense of direction, having to trust my gut on which path actually led back to civilization. But here's the thing‚Äîeach tiny disaster you survive makes you realize you're more capable than you thought. That flat tire on a back road in Mayo? Fixed it myself with help from a farmer who barely spoke English but communicated entirely through gestures. Ireland doesn't really coddle you, that's for sure, but it throws situations at you and watches you figure it out... and somehow you do !

Professional Skills Developed

Working for a few months in a restaurant (which is a job I have never done in France before) in a new place you've just arrived, with irish coworkers AND from all over the world, you have to develop many skills in order to have a great experience. The first one if of course teamwork, especially in this field, you can't succeed alone and you HAVE to communicate, even if at the start it's everyone communicating in half-English, half-gestures. I learned to read people fast : who needs backup without asking, when to jump in, when to stay in your lane, the cultural sensitivity stops being abstract when you're working alongside Irish locals and Polish delivery drivers. Another thing about customer service in Ireland : it's not the scripted French politeness, it's genuine banter while you're exhausted and they're drunk, staying patient when they can't decide what they want, handling complaints without taking them personally. This builds resilience and emotional intelligence you can't learn in school.

Practical Considerations

Transportation

Driving on the left is genuinely terrifying for about 48 hours, then your brain rewires and it's fine. Renting a car gives you freedom that public transport simply cannot match‚Äîthose hidden gems and random detours are only accessible with your own wheels. While parking in cities is a nightmare and expensive, rural let you park literally anywhere that isn't actively someone's driveway. The roads are absurdly narrow, you will meet tractors and sheep, you will have to reverse 200 meters because two cars can't pass, accept this reality and don't stress. Roundabouts everywhere, get comfortable with them fast. Navigation apps work but locals' directions are often better, even when they sound insane like "turn at the blue house, you'll know it.

üèïÔ∏è

Accommodation

Try to mix everything : camping when weather permits and you want to save money, hostels when you need humans and showers, B&Bs when you want actual comfort and a massive breakfast. I slept in parking lots, barns (with permission of course !), official campsites, and one genuinely sketchy hostel I left after one night. Flexibility is crucial because booking everything ahead kills spontaneity, but having zero plan means you might end up scrambling at 9pm. Cash-only places are common, especially small campings and family-run B&S, so that cash stash is essential.

üí∞

Budget Tips

Ireland isn't cheap, accept that upfront. Camping and self-catering saves massive money‚Äîsupermarket meals versus restaurant meals is like ‚Ç¨5 versus ‚Ç¨20. Always carry cash because ATMs get sparse and fees add up. Pubs are expensive but skipping them means missing the culture, so budget for a few pints per week minimum. Free camping spots exist but require research and boldness. Petrol is expensive, factor that into your driving costs.

Tech & Apps

Google Maps offline download saved me repeatedly when signal vanished. I kept a voice note journal while driving that captured thoughts I'd have forgotten otherwise. Spotify offline playlists for those long solo drives. WhatsApp for staying connected with family without international charges destroying me. Honestly didn't need much else...

Final Thoughts

If you're a student considering international experiences, stop overthinking it and just go. Yes it's scary, yes you'll mess up, yes there will be moments you want to quit and go home (I had plenty to be fair). But even the hard moments, become the stories that define the trip. The value isn't in doing everything perfectly, it's in showing up and being open to whatever happens. Solo travel specifically forces growth that group travel never will. There's no one to hide behind, no one to make decisions for you, no safety net when things go wrong except your own resourcefulness. It's uncomfortable and lonely sometimes, but that discomfort is where you actually learn who you are outside your normal context. Ireland's smallness and friendliness makes it an ideal place for this challenging enough to push you, safe enough that you won't genuinely get lost forever.
